About the Project

Understanding Parkinson’s Disease
Parkinson’s disease (PD) is a progressive neurological disorder that primarily affects movement. It occurs due to the gradual degeneration of dopamine-producing neurons in the brain, specifically in the substantia nigra region. As dopamine levels decline, individuals experience symptoms such as tremors, muscle stiffness, bradykinesia (slowness of movement), and postural instability. While PD is commonly associated with aging, early-onset cases exist, making early detection critical for better management.

Why Early Detection Matters

Currently, there is no cure for Parkinson’s disease, but early detection and intervention can slow its progression and improve patients’ quality of life. Early symptoms, such as slight tremors, minor balance issues, and subtle changes in handwriting, are often overlooked.
With advancements in hardware-based monitoring and machine learning, we can now detect these early warning signs more effectively. Our project aims to bridge this gap by providing real-time monitoring and predictive analysis for early-stage Parkinson’s detection. By using ESP32, MPU6050, BMP280, HW827, and a GPS module, our system collects and analyzes movement patterns, heart rate, and other vital signs. With the integration of machine learning models, we enhance detection accuracy and provide meaningful insights to healthcare professionals. This project not only contributes to early diagnosis but also enables continuous monitoring, reducing the risks associated with Parkinson’s-related falls and complications.

The Impact of Parkinson’s Disease

  • Global Prevalence: Over 10 million people worldwide are living with Parkinson’s disease.
  • Reduced Quality of Life: Patients face difficulties in daily activities, mobility issues, and increased fall risks.
  • Healthcare Burden: The economic and social costs of PD treatment are significant, affecting families and healthcare systems.
  • Mental & Emotional Challenges: Depression, anxiety, and cognitive decline are common among PD patients.
